# Email #29: You Already Have the Knowledge Gene

**Subject Line:** The curiosity you had as a child didn't disappear — it was buried

---

Think about a 3-year-old.

They ask "why" constantly. They touch everything. They fall, get up, try again — endlessly, without embarrassment. They learn to walk, to talk, to understand an entire world in just a few years.

At a rate that no adult can match.

Then they go to school. The questions slow down. The curiosity gets redirected into compliance. The love of learning gets gradually replaced with a fear of being wrong.

And by university — the very institution supposedly built for learning — many students have lost contact with the instinct that made them the most capable learners they'd ever be.

---

**The Knowledge Gene argues:**

Human beings are biologically built for learning. Curiosity, imitation, sense-making, and exploration are not personality traits — they're survival mechanisms encoded into our DNA.

The struggle modern students have with learning is not a deficiency of intelligence or drive. It's a misalignment between how we're built to learn — through exploration, context, genuine questions, and real application — and how we're required to learn in school — passively, linearly, under constant threat of judgment.

Your brain wants to learn. School taught it to resist *this particular format* of learning.

**The bridge back to curiosity:**

Find one thing — just one — about your least favourite subject that actually interests you.

A strange application of the theory. An unexpected connection to something you care about. An open question that nobody has fully answered.

Start there. Not because the whole subject will suddenly become fascinating. But because curiosity is a thread. Pull it, and more follows.
