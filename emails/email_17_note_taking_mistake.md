# Email #17: The Note-Taking Mistake Costing You Understanding

**Subject Line:** Your notes aren't the problem — what you do after is

---

Your notes aren't the problem. What you do — or don't do — with them after you write them is.

---

Most students take notes in lectures and never engage with them again until the night before the exam.

At that point, the notes become a desperate life raft — frantically useful, and completely insufficient.

But the real failure happened much earlier.

The act of note-taking became a performance: write things down to *feel* like you're learning, not to *actually build* understanding. The notebook fills up. The comprehension doesn't.

---

**Sönke Ahrens, in *How to Take Smart Notes*, reframes the entire purpose of notes:**

A note is only valuable when it's connected to other ideas.

When you write a note and it lives in isolation — one page in a notebook, never revisited, never linked to anything else — it's essentially worthless for understanding. It's an archive, not a thinking tool.

But when a note connects to something you already know — when it challenges, supports, or extends another idea — it becomes part of your thinking. It builds understanding that stays.

The key question after every lecture isn't: *"What did I write down?"*

It's: *"What does this connect to? What does this change? What questions does this raise?"*

**Try this after your next lecture:**

Read through your notes once. Then close them and ask:
- What does this remind me of?
- How does this change something I thought I knew?
- What question is this material answering?

Write those connections down. That's the note that builds understanding. The rest is just transcription.
