# Email #6: Effort Without Direction Is Just Exhaustion

**Subject Line:** Working hard on the wrong things is worse than not working at all

---

Working hard on the wrong things is worse than not working at all — because at least then, you wouldn't be tired.

---

I've watched students carry heavy bags, fill thick notebooks, attend every lecture, and still fail.

Not because they didn't work. Because they worked without ever asking: *"What actually matters here?"*

They were busy. But they weren't effective.

---

**Peter Drucker spent his career studying this.**

In *The Effective Executive*, he makes an argument that most students have never had applied to their own life:

Effectiveness is not doing more things. It's doing the *right* things — and relentlessly cutting everything else.

He calls it "contribution" — constantly asking, "What is the highest-value use of my time and energy right now?" Not what feels productive. Not what's comfortable. What actually moves the result.

For a student: Do you know your exam format cold? Do you know your highest-weighted topics? Do you know which of your study activities directly improve your exam score — and which ones just make you feel like you're working?

Most students don't. So their effort scatters.

**The fix:**

Write down the 3 most high-value activities for your next exam. Not 10 — three.

Then ask: "Am I spending 80% of my study time on these three — or am I busy doing things that feel important but aren't critical?"

Redirect your effort. Cut ruthlessly. The results follow effort that's aimed at the right target.
