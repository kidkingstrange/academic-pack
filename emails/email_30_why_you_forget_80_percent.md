# Email #30: Why You Forget 80% of What You Study

**Subject Line:** You're not bad at remembering. You're bad at reviewing. Different problem.

---

You're not bad at remembering. You're bad at reviewing. And those are two completely different problems — with completely different solutions.

---

Imagine filling a bucket with water — but the bucket has holes. Every hour, water leaks out.

Most students respond by pouring more water in: more studying, more reading, more notes. But the bucket never stays full. Not because they're not adding enough — because nobody fixed the holes.

The holes are not a defect. They're a feature.

Your brain deliberately discards information it hasn't been signalled to retain. And the signal it looks for is simple: *use*. If you encounter a piece of information once and never engage with it again, your brain treats it as irrelevant and lets it go.

This is why a student can spend 6 hours studying and recall almost nothing three days later. The input happened. The signal to retain it never did.

---

**From *Learning How to Learn*:**

Memory consolidation — moving information from short-term into long-term storage — happens most efficiently during sleep and during spaced review. Your brain asks: "Has this been needed more than once? Has it been retrieved under pressure?" If yes, the pathway strengthens. If no, it prunes.

The student who studies something once — even for hours — loses most of it within a week. Not because they're forgetful. Because the system never received the signal to keep it.

**Fix the holes — starting today:**

The three-step review cycle:
1. Review within 24 hours of learning (recall from memory, not re-reading)
2. Review 3 days later (self-test without notes)
3. Review 1 week later (past exam questions on that topic)

This signals to your brain: this material has been needed three times. Keep it.

Over a semester done consistently, you don't arrive at exam week trying to remember — you arrive having already remembered for months.
