In the 1880s, a German psychologist named Ebbinghaus ran an experiment on himself — memorising lists of nonsense syllables, then measuring how quickly he forgot them.

What he found became the "forgetting curve": without review, we forget 50% of new information within a day. Nearly 80% within a week.

But he also discovered the solution — and it's the part that most students never implement.

---

**From *Learning How to Learn*:**

Spaced repetition — reviewing material at precisely calibrated, increasing intervals — doesn't just slow forgetting. At the right intervals, it nearly *stops* it.

The first review: within 24 hours of learning.
The second review: 3 days after the first.
The third review: 7 days after the second.

Each review at the right interval strengthens the memory trace exponentially. By the time your exam arrives, the material isn't freshly memorised — it's deeply encoded.

The students who use this don't cram the night before. They can't — because they've already known it for weeks.

**Your action starting today:**

After every lecture, spend 10 minutes reviewing — not reading, but *recalling*. Close your notes and write what you remember.

Three days later, do it again without looking.

One week later, test yourself with past exam questions on that topic.

This three-step cycle, done consistently, will transform what you retain.
