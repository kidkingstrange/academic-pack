# Email #36: Hard Work Alone Won't Save You

**Subject Line:** Barbara Oakley couldn't do maths. Then she learned how the brain actually changes.

---

Barbara Oakley failed maths in school. She avoided anything quantitative for most of her early adult life — convinced she simply wasn't a "maths person."

Then, in her 30s, she decided to start from scratch.

What she discovered changed not just her own brain — but her entire understanding of what human beings are capable of learning.

---

**From *Mindshift*:**

The brain is not fixed. It is plastic.

New wiring can be built where none existed before — at any age, across any subject, regardless of prior failure. But neuroplasticity doesn't respond to effort alone. It responds to *the right kind* of effort.

The students who transform academically are not simply the ones who work harder. They are the ones who change their *approach* to difficulty.

Specifically: they lean into confusion instead of retreating from it. They treat struggle as signal — a sign that new wiring is being built — rather than as evidence that they don't belong.

They also do something most students never do: they question the story they've been telling themselves about what they're "not good at."

---

**The most damaging academic belief:**

*"I'm just not good at [subject]."*

This feels like self-awareness. It's actually a fixed decision about a brain that hasn't been given the right approach yet.

Oakley wasn't bad at maths. She had been taught maths badly, using methods that didn't match how her brain processed information — and she internalised the failure as a personal verdict.

**One honest question for today:**

What is the subject or skill you've decided you're "just not good at"?

Now ask: *"Have I ever tried a fundamentally different approach to learning this — or have I only repeated the same approach that failed, but with more effort?"*

If the honest answer is "same approach, more effort" — the issue has never been your ability. It's been your method.
