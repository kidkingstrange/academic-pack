There's a piece of mathematics that most students never apply to themselves:

A student who improves by 1% every day for one year doesn't improve by 365%.

**Teresa Amabile's research in *The Progress Principle* explains why this works:**

The most powerful motivator for sustained, long-term performance is not a single breakthrough. It's the daily, consistent experience of small forward movement.

People who see themselves making progress — even tiny, incremental progress — sustain effort longer, perform better under pressure, and maintain higher levels of engagement than those waiting for a dramatic turning point.

The turning point doesn't come. The compound growth does.

**The student who reviews for 30 minutes every day** isn't just learning more material.

They're building a self-perception as someone who learns consistently. That identity change compounds over time into results that look extraordinary from the outside but feel entirely natural from the inside.

**One commitment — starting today:**

Choose one small, daily study action that is so small it's almost embarrassing to not do it.

Not a 3-hour session. Not a full week's revision. Something tiny and undeniable:
- 20 minutes of active recall after dinner
- 10 flashcard repetitions every morning
- One past exam question before bed

Do it every day for 30 days. Watch what happens to your confidence, your retention, and your relationship with your own studying.
