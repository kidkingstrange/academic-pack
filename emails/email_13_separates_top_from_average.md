# Email #13: What Separates Top Students From Average Ones

**Subject Line:** The gap between average and excellent isn't talent — it's one habit

---

The gap between average and excellent isn't talent. It's strategy — and strategy is learnable.

---

Scott Young studied people who mastered complex skills at remarkable speed — across languages, mathematics, coding, and more.

He found one consistent differentiator that separated rapid learners from slow ones. It wasn't IQ. It wasn't hours studied. It wasn't natural ability.

It was this: top performers *thought about how they were thinking*.

They asked themselves:
- "Is what I'm doing actually working?"
- "What's the fastest path to mastering this specific thing?"
- "What's the biggest obstacle between me and the result I need?"

Average students study. Top students study — *and* analyse how they're studying.

---

**From *Ultralearning*:**

Young calls this metalearning — spending time, before and during any learning project, understanding the structure of what you're learning, the most efficient methods for learning it, and the biggest obstacles you'll face.

This meta-level thinking is almost never taught in school. Which is exactly why the students who stumble onto it seem to leap ahead of everyone else — suddenly and dramatically.

They didn't get smarter. They got strategic.

**10 minutes that will change your next exam cycle:**

Before you study anything this week, spend 10 minutes answering these three questions:

1. What exactly am I trying to master? (Be specific — not "chemistry" but "organic reaction mechanisms")
2. What's the most efficient path to that mastery? (Past exam questions? Practice problems? Concept maps?)
3. What's been blocking me so far? (Distraction? Misunderstanding? No review system?)

This 10-minute session will direct the next 10 hours of study more effectively than any amount of unplanned grinding.
