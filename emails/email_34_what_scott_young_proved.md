Scott Young completed MIT's 4-year computer science curriculum in 12 months.

No lectures. No campus. No tuition. And he passed every exam.

The most important part of that story is not the 12 months.

---

**From *Ultralearning*:**

Young didn't start by studying. He started by mapping.

Before touching a single subject, he spent time understanding the full territory: What are all the things I need to learn? What are the most efficient methods for each? What are the biggest obstacles? What does success actually look like for each component?

This metalearning phase — understanding the structure of the learning before beginning — is almost universally skipped by students who dive straight into content.

The result: weeks of studying the wrong things, in the wrong order, with the wrong methods. Then cramming the night before because the real priorities were never identified.

**Then he studied directly:**

No reading about problem-solving. Actually solving problems.
No watching others explain. Explaining it back from memory.
No passive review. Active retrieval under exam-like conditions.

Every hour was aimed at the exact performance he needed to demonstrate.
