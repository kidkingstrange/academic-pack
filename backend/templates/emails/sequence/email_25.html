Tony Buzan spent his career studying how the human brain actually processes and stores information. His conclusion: the linear, sequential notes students have been using for centuries are structurally incompatible with how the brain works.

The brain doesn't think in lists. It thinks in networks — connecting concepts to other concepts, ideas to images, memories to associations.

Every time you write a linear list, you're forcing your networked brain into a flat, one-dimensional format. Information goes in. But understanding — the kind built from connections — stays thin.

 

From Mind Map Mastery:

A mind map places the central concept at the centre and branches outward — connecting related ideas, sub-topics, and supporting details using colour, imagery, and spatial relationships.

This radial structure mirrors how your brain actually stores information. The result: better comprehension and better recall — because you're encoding in the same format the brain uses to retrieve.

Try this for one concept today:

Take a topic you're currently studying. Without opening your notes, draw it from memory:
- Central concept in the middle, circled
- 5 main branches for the key sub-topics
- Details branching from each main branch
- Use colour if you have it

The gaps you discover in your map are the exact things you need to study next. The map becomes your study diagnostic.
