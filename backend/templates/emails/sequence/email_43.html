There's a point beyond which more studying doesn't improve your performance.

It destroys it.

And most students in exam season cross that line without realising it.

 

Three weeks from exams. A student starts pulling longer hours — 10, 12, 14 hours of study per day.

Week 2, they're exhausted. Retaining almost nothing.

Making errors they wouldn't make on a normal day. Getting more anxious, not less.

They think: "I just need to push harder."

But they've crossed the threshold. On the other side of that threshold, effort produces negative returns — and pushing harder makes it worse, not better.

 

Jim Loehr and Tony Schwartz explain the mechanism in The Power of Full Engagement:

High performance requires alternating between the performance zone — where you're working — and the recovery zone — where you're restoring.

When you stay in the performance zone too long without deliberate recovery, your capacity doesn't stay constant. It shrinks.

Decision quality deteriorates. Memory consolidation slows (it happens primarily during sleep).

Emotional regulation collapses. Creativity disappears.

Elite performers don't perform for longer. They recover smarter.

Rest, sleep, movement, and genuine breaks are not rewards for working hard. They are tools of high performance.

The counterintuitive prescription for exam season:

If you're currently in a marathon study sprint:

  • 8 hours of real sleep tonight — not negotiable
  • A 20-minute walk tomorrow morning
  • Hard stop at a defined time in the evening, every evening
  • 5-minute breaks every 45–50 minutes of study

These aren't concessions. They are the conditions under which your studying actually produces retention.

Without recovery, you're filling the bucket with holes in it — again.
