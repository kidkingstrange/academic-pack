A student fails two courses in a row.

The instinctive conclusion — theirs, their family's, sometimes even their lecturer's — is: "They're not smart enough. They don't work hard enough. They're just not cut out for this."

But what if that diagnosis is completely wrong?

What if the real cause is upstream — in the structure of how they're studying, not in their capacity to succeed?

 

Donella Meadows, in Thinking in Systems, makes a principle clear:

The outcomes we see in any system are a product of the system's structure — not the intentions or intelligence of the people inside it.

If a student consistently produces poor results despite genuine effort, the first question is not: "What is wrong with them?" The first question is: "What is wrong with the structure they're operating in?"

No clear study schedule. Passive learning methods.

No review cycle. No accountability.

Environment full of distractions. No map of what matters most in each course.

These are structural problems. And structural problems produce structural outcomes — consistently, regardless of intelligence or effort.

Blaming yourself for your grades is like blaming the river for flooding when the upstream dam has been broken for months.

The grades are feedback. Not a verdict on your intelligence — feedback on your current system.

The fix is not to try harder in the same structure. The fix is to change the structure.

What to audit today:

Look at your last difficult exam result. Don't ask: "Why did I fail?"

Ask: "What was happening upstream — in my daily study habits, my environment, my review system, my preparation process — that made this outcome almost inevitable?"

Map the structural causes. Even identifying two or three of them gives you a real place to start.

The student hasn't changed. The structure has. And the outputs follow.
