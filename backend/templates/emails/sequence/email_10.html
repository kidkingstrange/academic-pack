Your brain doesn't record information like a video camera. It builds it, piece by piece, like a scaffold.

And most students are trying to build the top floor without laying the foundation.

 

Here's something your lecturers probably never explained:

Memory isn't stored as one single piece. It's stored as a web of connections — and the strength of those connections depends entirely on how many times, and in how many ways, you've encountered and actively used that information.

This is why you can remember a song you haven't heard in 10 years, but can't recall what you studied last Tuesday.

The song fired across hundreds of emotional connections over years. Last Tuesday's notes fired once — passively — and were never revisited.

 

Barbara Oakley explains this in A Mind for Numbers:

Learning happens through the building of "chunks" — compact neural packages of information that become automatic with practice.

A chunk isn't formed by reading. It's formed by:
1.

Understanding the concept deeply
2. Practising it actively
3.

Retrieving it repeatedly

Until the pathway becomes so well-worn that it fires automatically — like knowing how to ride a bike without thinking about balance.

Most students try to skip steps 2 and 3. They read without practising, note without retrieving, and wonder why nothing sticks.

The test for whether you've built a chunk:

Can you explain this concept clearly, in your own words, without looking at your notes?

If yes — the chunk is forming.
If no — you haven't finished learning it yet. Keep going.

Do this for every concept after every lecture. It's the difference between information that fades and knowledge that stays.
