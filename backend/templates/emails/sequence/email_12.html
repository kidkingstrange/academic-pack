A mentor is not someone who does the work for you. They're someone who shows you which work to do — and makes sure you actually do it.

That distinction changes everything.

 

Peter Drucker worked with the most effective executives in the world. And he observed something that surprised even him:

Even the most brilliant, experienced people were consistently wrong about their own strengths and weaknesses.

Not a little wrong. Significantly wrong.

They had blind spots they couldn't see — not because they weren't intelligent, but because no one can accurately assess themselves from inside their own perspective. We see what we want to see.

We repeat what feels familiar. We avoid what makes us uncomfortable — even when that's exactly what we need.

 

From Managing Oneself (Drucker):

The prescription for this is structured external feedback — honest, timely, from someone outside your blind spots.

Drucker observed that throughout history, every great performer — musicians, soldiers, scholars, executives — had someone outside themselves providing that external mirror.

Not someone who told them what they wanted to hear. Someone who told them what they needed to hear, clearly and specifically.

The mentor doesn't add intelligence. They add visibility. They help you see what you can't see from where you're standing.

Be honest with yourself right now:

Do you have anyone in your academic life who gives you direct, honest, structured feedback on how you're studying — not just encouragement?

Someone who says: "You're spending time on the wrong topics." Or: "The way you're taking notes is why you're not retaining." Or: "This is why your exam answers aren't landing, and here's how to fix it."

If the answer is no — you're navigating with one eye closed.
