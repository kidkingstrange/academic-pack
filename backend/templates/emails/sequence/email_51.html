Peter Drucker, in The Effective Executive, introduces the concept of opportunity cost:

Every decision not to invest in your own development is implicitly a decision to stay exactly where you are.

There is no neutral option. You are always either building or stagnating. Every week, you are either compounding growth or compounding drift.

The students who accelerate fastest are not always the ones with the most resources. They're the ones who decided — under tight constraints, under real pressure — that their development was a priority.

And that decision compounded.

 

The question is not whether you can afford the mentorship.

The real question is: Can you afford another semester of the same results?

Calculate it honestly. The grade you're currently on track for.

The opportunity that requires a different grade. The gap between them.

The cost of that gap in concrete terms — career, self-respect, future options.

Compare that to the cost of the mentorship.

The calculation usually looks completely different when you include the hidden costs of staying stuck.
