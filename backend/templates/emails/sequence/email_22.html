In 2001, a critical care specialist at Johns Hopkins introduced a 5-item checklist for a single medical procedure in the ICU.

Infection rates dropped by 66%.

Across hospitals in Michigan alone, it was estimated to prevent 43 deaths per ICU per year.

Five items. Massive impact.

 

The doctors weren't ignorant. They knew every step. But under pressure — with competing demands, interruptions, and the weight of dozens of decisions — even world-class experts skipped steps.

Not from negligence. From cognitive overload.

Atul Gawande chronicles this in The Checklist Manifesto: checklists are not for beginners. They are for experts operating under complexity. They externalise memory so the brain can focus on judgement, not recall.

Now apply this to your academic life:

How often do you go into a study session without a clear plan?
How often do you finish a revision cycle unsure whether you've actually covered the key areas?
How often does exam preparation feel reactive instead of systematic?

These are not intelligence problems. They are process problems. And process problems are solved by — you guessed it — process tools.

Your pre-study checklist (try tonight):

☐ Session goal written down (specific topic, not "study chemistry")
☐ Phone in another room
☐ Materials ready before sitting down
☐ Timer set (25 or 45 minutes)

Your post-study checklist:

☐ Self-test: what can I recall without notes?
☐ Gaps identified: what do I still not understand?
☐ Next session: what's the priority?

Run this for one week. Watch how much more intentional your studying becomes — not because you're working harder, but because you've removed the friction of figuring it out each time.
