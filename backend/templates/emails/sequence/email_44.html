Hey {{ name }},

Close your eyes for a moment.

Picture yourself three years from now — having studied with real intention this entire time. Having built the skills, the habits, the academic record you're capable of.

Who is that person?

What do they know? What did they earn?

Who do they know? What opportunities are available to them?

Hold that picture clearly.

 

Covey describes a principle he calls "beginning with the end in mind" — the act of clearly defining the outcome before you begin the journey toward it.

Not as wishful thinking. As navigational clarity.

When you know precisely who you're becoming, every daily decision gets simpler: "Does this action move me toward or away from that person?"

From The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People, Habit 2: All things are created twice — first in the mind, then in the physical world.

The student who graduates with distinction first graduated in their imagination. They held that picture through every difficult week, every moment when quitting would have been easier, every Friday when friends were out and they were studying.

The picture wasn't a fantasy. It was a compass.

 

Without a clear end in mind, your effort is scattered.

Some weeks you're working toward your future. Some weeks you're managing today's discomfort. The difference between them is often invisible from the inside.

With a clear picture, you know the difference. And you can choose which one you're doing.

Write this down right now — specific, not vague:

"Who am I academically and professionally in 3 years if everything goes the way I'm working toward?"

Include: GPA or degree class. Career direction.

Key skills. The kind of professional you are.

The doors that are open.

Keep this somewhere visible. Read it when studying feels purposeless. Let the end in mind give the present its meaning.

 

In the mentorship, one of the first exercises is building your 3-year vision — and then working backwards to design the semester, the month, the week, and the day that leads there.

Purpose-driven study is not just more motivating. It's more effective — because you always know why you're doing what you're doing.
