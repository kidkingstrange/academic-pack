You've been studying the wrong way. And the worst part? It feels like it's working.

 

Every student has done this: sit down, read through notes, highlight the important parts, maybe rewrite a few key points. Close the book feeling like you've done solid work.

Then the exam comes. And it's like you never studied at all.

This is called the fluency illusion.

Because the material looked familiar when you read it, your brain tricked you into thinking you knew it. But recognition is not recall. And exams test recall.

 

The research is clear:

From Learning How to Learn (Oakley and Sejnowski) — retrieval practice, the act of pulling information from memory without looking at your notes, is the single most powerful study technique available.

More powerful than rereading.
More powerful than highlighting.
More powerful than rewriting notes.

Every time you retrieve something from memory, you strengthen the neural pathway. Every time you reread, you just confirm an illusion.

The fix — starting tonight:

After every study session, close your materials. Write down everything you can remember.

No notes. Don't check first.

It will be uncomfortable. You will remember less than you expect. That discomfort is the signal that real learning is happening.

Do this for two weeks and compare your exam confidence to what it's been before. It will be unrecognisable.
